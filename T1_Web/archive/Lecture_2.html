Comparative Advantage

Having a lower opportunity cost is what we call comparative advantage. And here, Andrew has a comparative advantage in carrots, and Molly has a comparative advantage in spinach. As you might have seen before, there is an important mathematical realization here. When one farmer‚Äôs opportunity cost is lower in one crop, the other farmer‚Äôs opportunity cost will always be lower in the other.

So Molly realizes that maybe there‚Äôs something to this idea, that maybe the guild should be minimizing their joint opportunity cost. So she and Andrew decide to specialize in what each farmer is relatively most efficient at growing, what they have a comparative advantage in. So Molly grows only spinach, growing 45 . And Andrew grows only carrots, growing 4 .

And we can check our work here by checking what happens to our production when we specialize in the good we don't have the comparative advantage in. And our production point is inside the guild's equal-split PPF, so we've correctly chosen the specialization according to comparative advantage.

So without changing any technology, simply by choosing their crop according to their comparative advantage, the local farmers guild was able to exceed the frontier. Its a more general truth that so long as we're not too close to the edges, we can exceed any point on the PPF with specialization. The limit to this is when one farmer produces all of the good they don't have a comparative advantage in.
