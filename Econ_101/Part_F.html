Part F | Buyers

Part F asks the question, "what we should do when we can't have everything"? To do this we build a model of buyer's income using Factor Markets and the Labor Market. This links the market for Final Goods to buyer's labor income. We use this income along with prices in the final goods market to build the Budget Constraint. Buyers aim to maximize their preferences, which we model by ranking all possible alternatives and assigning higher ranked alternatives a higher number. This ranking using numbers is what we call Utility. We used a contour plot to show that an Indifference Curve walks us across equally preferred goods, like we're walking around a mountain without gaining or losing elevation. The solution to the Consumer's Problem maximizes Utility when we have climbed to the highest indifference curve while remaining within the budget constraint. This happens when the Marginal Rate of Substitution equals the Marginal Rate of Transformation.

This closes out the semester! This course has charted a path through economic theory relying on our models of why buyers and sellers choose to interact. In Part A we started by building a model of the gains from trade using Opportunity Cost and Specialization. In Part B we introduced markets as a way to coordinate where on the PPF to live and used buyers' preferences with the demand curve and sellers' costs with the supply curve to give us equilibrium. In Part C and Part D we showed how markets can fail and how governments can fix market failures. Then we developed a more sophisticated model of the supply curve using the incentives faced by sellers. Part F wraps up this course through economic theory by doing a similar thing for buyers, generating the demand curve from the incentives faced by buyers.
